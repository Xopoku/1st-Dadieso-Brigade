Brief History of the Girls' Brigade

The Girls brigade was founded by Sunday school union in United Kingdom in the year 1893. The founder of Girls Brigade was LATE MRS MARGARET BEAUTY. Girls Brigade was shared into three branches: Girls brigade which started in 1893, Girls Guidry which started in 1900, and Girls life brigade which started in 1902. In 10th June 1968 the brigade week of prayer started and it is the date to the memorial of the joining of the three organization which is celebrated and observed every 10th June in the year.

meaning of the girls' Brigade

It is an international non-denominational organization founded by the Sunday school union in United Kingdom in the year 1893. It was formed with two pillars; Bible Study and Physical Training.

HISTORY OF GIRLS’ BRIGADE GHANA

The Girls’ Brigade came to Ghana in the year 1955 through the efforts of the late Mrs. Gladys Dagadu who was the National President of Ghana Girls’ Brigade. The International Headquarters sent a Training Officer to Ghana in the person of Miss Shanks to assist in the extension work. The first Ghanaian Company – preparations as well as training were held at the Methodist Mission House in James Town, Accra with the help of the 1st Accra Company of the Boys’ Brigade which was already in existence.

In 1955 the 1st Accra Company which was obviously the 1st Gold Coast Company was officially inaugurated at the Wesley Church, Zion Street, Accra by the late Rev. J.K. Clegg and the late J.E. Addo. We had cadets aged between six and eight years, seniors aged between ten and fourteen and Pioneers aged above fifteen years. Later, other companies spread into the Country notably the 1st Larteh Company, 1st Obuasi, 2nd Accra, 3rd Accra, 1st Sekondi, 1st Cape Coast and 1st Kumasi. This pioneering work was undertaken through the hard work of the late Mrs. Koram and Miss Shanks, a Scottish Brigade officer who was then teaching at Akropong. By 1957 the Girls’ Life Brigade as it was known had been recognized as a youth movement of considerable standing. No doubt in the celebrations marking Ghana’s Independence in 1957 the Girls’ Life Brigade took part in this historic parade at the Accra Sports Stadium.

It became necessary after Independence, to establish a National Council to steer the affairs of the Brigade. Members of the Council came from the Churches that had Brigade Companies and Youth and Women’s Fellowship. The Presbyterian Church was represented by Miss Bachler, the Anglican Church by Miss Pearson, the Methodist by Rev. J.K. Clegg, the E.P. Church by Mrs. Vivian Hazel, the Methodist Women’s Work by Mrs. J.O. Noom, the Christian Council by the then General Secretary, Rev. T.A. Osei, and Miss Shanks, who was then the Secretary for the Council.

Programme was slow in the early days with isolated companies set up in Kumasi, Sekondi and Obuasi. In 1961 moves were made to form a Council under the Chairmanship of Mrs. Dagadu but this too went through a difficult period until 1963 when the Council was reformed and a constitution written. By then there were eleven (11) companies established in the country.

Much of the development may be attributed to the late Mrs. R.M. Creedy, a Methodist Missionary, who worked for a number of years as honorary National Secretary, Miss Daphine Fletcher and Miss Kathleen McDonald both workers in Nigeria, visited Ghana on separate occasions to encourage and help in training. Miss H.M. Rae International Secretary also visited Ghana.

Further work in the Brigade was done by the late Mrs. Rosemary Creedy who struggled under difficult conditions for 12 years until she was called for higher service about some years ago. Her work during these twelve years showed results through the great expansion that took place. We appreciated her massive work and are thankful to God for providing her. May she rest in peace.

We recall the wonderful assistance we have received from Miss Hilda Rae, the retired Overseas Secretary, whose good work is currently being continued by Miss Sandra Kimber who is now the Atlantic Secretary. We can also never forget Joyce and Basil Chaplain who helped in the formation of the Kumasi companies. In 1969 Mrs. Nancy Quansah became the first Ghanaian Training Officer working in a voluntary capacity until 1978 when she was officially appointed as the National Organizer/Secretary.

Since her departure from the scene, Mrs. Nancy Quansah has been working as National Organizer/Secretary under whose guidance we hope to continue expansion work, particularly in the Volta Region. We shall be doing a great deal of injustice if we fail to record on thanks to all our friends without whose sustained help little could have been achieved over the past years.

Nancy joined the Girls’ Brigade in Accra in 1955 as a Lieutenant. In 1957, she was transferred to Takoradi and started a company there. In 1967 she returned to Accra and took over as Captain of the 1st Accra Company from Mrs. Dagadu who was then the National President.

The first Girls’ Brigade International Council Meetings were held in London in 1968 and Nancy attended as Ghana’s representative. She took full advantage of her journey to stayed on for further training at International Headquarters.

The work of a National Organizer/Secretary is very varied. Responsible for every part of Ghana where Brigade is functioning; the work entails a great future leaders for extension and helping to increase the efficiency of the present officers. She organizes National Training Courses, helps the Regional Commissioners to hold their own local courses and visits companies regularly.

As Secretary, she is responsible for all the office work connected with the running of the National Council, keeping records and maintaining communication with the company officer. Hopes for the future include extension of the work into the Volta Region and seeking to establish more vocational training opportunities, i.e. training in practical skills like sewing and other handicrafts, for girls who cannot go to secondary school and who cannot find work. One scheme to help the unemployed girl was set up in Koforidua where they have a canteen, which caters for ten schools in the Betom area. It is hoped that other companies will take up similar projects.

Ghana became a full member of the International Council in 1973. Presently, the Girls’ Brigade in Ghana is a lively organization working mostly within the Methodist, Presbyterian and Anglican Churches. The 3rd B.I.C. of the Girls’ Brigade was held in Cayman Island and Miss Beatrice B. Oduro represented the Ghana Girls’ Brigade. After the conference she came to London Headquarters to acquaint herself with the Brigade family for six weeks.

Ghana is divided into Regions and each Region has a Brigade Commissioner and she represents her area on the Brigade National Council which meets fairly regularly. Each year they hold an Officers’ Conference and AGM which any officer may attend.
